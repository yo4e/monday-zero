# Genesis Mechanism Set

Generated without ranking. Similarity notes are provisional until a deliberate prior-art search.

## 1. Relay

A 5×5 race game. Each player begins with five stones on a home edge. Stones advance or move sideways; captures are diagonal and forward. After every move, all of a player's stones must remain connected by king-adjacency. Reach the opposite edge to win.

Core tension: local advance versus preserving the moving formation.

## 2. Span

A connection game in which a new stone must expand the bounding box of the friendly component it joins, or merge two friendly components. Each player attempts to connect assigned opposite edges.

Core tension: efficient expansion versus vulnerability to blocking.

## 3. Keystone

Players place or shift stones on a 5×5 board. A player wins by occupying the center and connecting it to two distinct board edges. Custodian captures remove a single enemy stone bracketed orthogonally.

Core tension: a visible structural goal complicated by reversible local control.

## 4. Echo Step

A shared runner moves across an initially empty 5×5 board, leaving visited cells blocked. Each move vector must be a rotation or reflection of the previous vector; reversal is forbidden. The player making the last legal move wins.

Core tension: shaping the geometry of future motion.

## 5. Counterweight

Players place stones on a 5×5 board. A placement changes a balance marker according to its distance and direction from the center. A player may complete a line only while the marker favors the opponent.

Core tension: building threats while lending tempo or leverage.

## 6. Tidepool

After placing a stone, the player shifts one complete row or column one cell toward the center, with edge pieces falling away. Form a connected group touching three edges.

Core tension: every constructive move also disturbs the board.

## 7. Ravel

Players grow paths from opposite corners. A new stone must touch exactly one friendly stone. Touching two friendly stones closes a loop and removes the smaller enclosed friendly branch.

Core tension: growth without premature self-contact.

## 8. Pulse

A placement sends a one-cell orthogonal pulse. The first occupied stone in each direction changes allegiance; empty space stops nothing, but the board edge does. Control all four cells adjacent to the center to win.

Core tension: influence propagates beyond the move's location.

## 9. Hinge

Each player controls four two-cell pieces on a 4×4 board. A piece moves only by rotating ninety degrees around either occupied end. Connect two assigned edges with occupied cells.

Core tension: movement is reversible but spatially constrained.

## 10. Mosaic

Players alternate colors rather than owning a fixed color. On a turn, a player places the current color, then chooses the color the opponent must place. Creating a monochrome 2×2 is illegal. Complete a five-cell orthogonal path in either color to win.

Core tension: control of tempo and color is separated from ownership.

## 11. Rift

Players place walls and move a shared pawn. A wall must divide two orthogonally adjacent cells; the pawn then moves until blocked. When trapped, territory is assigned by which player's walls bound each reachable region.

Core tension: shared movement with privately authored geometry.

## 12. Thread

Players draw noncrossing segments between adjacent points on a square lattice. A new segment must extend an existing friendly endpoint, but a player may spend a turn cutting one enemy segment crossed by a newly completed friendly loop.

Core tension: connection, enclosure, and delayed removal.
